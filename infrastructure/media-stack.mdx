> The download tier has exactly one path to the internet: through the VPN. If the VPN drops, the container drops with it.

The homelab's media stack runs as a chain of LXCs on a dedicated VLAN. The shape is conventional — a download client, the `*arr` indexers, a library server — but with a hard rule that the download container cannot reach the internet except through a VPN tunnel.

## The chain

| Tier | Role | Why a container, why this VLAN |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `download-vpn` | Download client + VPN gateway | Outbound is gated by killswitch; LXC keeps it on the host's network stack so policy is enforceable at the kernel layer |
| `sonarr` / `radarr` | Indexers, automation | LXC; talks to `download-vpn` over the media VLAN, not the public internet |
| `plex` | Library / streaming | LXC; reads from a media library dataset on the cluster storage tier |

All four sit on the same VLAN (`media_svc` in the per-service scheme). The VLAN itself has no policy out to the wider infrastructure — it can reach the library and the download client and the internet (through the VPN tunnel only), and nothing else.

## Why VPN-locked

The download tier is the only piece of the homelab that initiates outbound traffic to consumer protocols. Treating it as untrusted and forcing its egress through a known-policy VPN tunnel:

- Keeps ISP-visible traffic on the homelab's terms
- Makes a leak detectable: any outbound packet from the LXC that didn't traverse the VPN interface gets dropped by netfilter, so "did this leak?" is answerable from logs
- Lets the `*arr` containers reach the same download client over the local VLAN without any of them needing the VPN themselves

## Container-first, not Docker

The download client and VPN gateway are commonly shipped as Docker images. The homelab runs them as LXC instead, configured by Ansible. Reasons are the same as the [LXC vs Docker decision tree](/infrastructure/lxc-vs-docker): kernel-namespace processes give predictable network handling, which is exactly what a killswitch needs.
